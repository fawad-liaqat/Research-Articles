The Behavioral Last Mile: Why 88% Digital Infrastructure Produces 60% Cash Economies

Fawad Liaqat March 2026 ~22 min read
Abstract

Background: Pakistan's digital payment infrastructure has achieved remarkable scale: 88% of retail transactions flow through digital channels by volume, Raast P2P transactions grew 8.7x in three years to 1.28 billion, and over one million merchants accept QR payments. Yet over 60% of economic activity remains cash-based, 98% of ATM transactions are cash withdrawals, and only 35% of adults are financially included. This paradox reveals a fundamental gap between supply-side infrastructure deployment and demand-side behavioral adoption.

Methods: This study employs comparative case study methodology using publicly available quantitative data from the State Bank of Pakistan Annual Payment Systems Review FY25 and Karandaaz Financial Inclusion Survey 2024. Pakistan serves as the primary case, with India's UPI system and Kenya's M-Pesa analyzed at equivalent infrastructure deployment stages (year 5 of system launch) to isolate behavioral variables explaining divergent adoption outcomes.

Results: The analysis identifies three compounding demand-side barriers that persist despite infrastructure deployment: trust deficit (only 9% of excluded adults trust banks, 8% trust mobile money), incentive misalignment (merchants face 1.5–2.5% discount fees that favor cash payment acceptance), and literacy friction (digital literacy lags smartphone penetration, particularly among women at 46% phone ownership vs. 82% for men, and in rural areas). These barriers compound multiplicatively rather than additively.

Conclusions: Five specific, precedent-backed interventions targeting behavioral barriers are proposed: eliminating merchant discount rates for transactions below PKR 5,000, redesigning payment systems for feature phone access via USSD/IVR, deploying trust intermediaries (agents) with aligned incentives, creating tax incentives for documented digital spending, and mandating Urdu-first interface design standards for financial applications.

Keywords: digital payments, financial inclusion, behavioral economics, Pakistan, Raast, cash persistence, UPI, M-Pesa

1. Introduction

In fiscal year 2025, 88% of Pakistan's retail payment transactions flowed through digital channels by volume (State Bank of Pakistan, 2025). Raast, the country's instant payment system launched in January 2021, processed 1.28 billion peer-to-peer transactions, a 157% increase over the previous year and an 8.7x increase from 147 million in FY23 (State Bank of Pakistan, 2025). Over one million merchants now accept QR code payments. Mobile banking app transactions reached 6.2 billion, growing 52% year over year. By any infrastructure metric, Pakistan's digital payment ecosystem is scaling rapidly. The branchless banking agent network has expanded to 731,814 agents. POS terminals exceed 195,000 units deployed across merchants. Branchless banking mobile app users reached 79.2 million, representing a 35% increase from the previous year.

Yet the behavioral reality tells a fundamentally different story. Over 60% of economic activity remains cash-based, representing a dominant economic transaction mode despite years of digital infrastructure investment. Of the 20,341 ATMs operating across the country, 98% of transactions are cash withdrawals (State Bank of Pakistan, 2025). This statistic is particularly revealing: even individuals who have overcome institutional distrust sufficiently to maintain bank accounts and possess debit cards still choose to convert their digital money to physical cash before engaging in economic activity. Financial inclusion stands at 35% of adults, up from 8% in 2013 but still among the lowest in South Asia (Karandaaz, 2025). An estimated 54 million bank deposit accounts hold balances below PKR 5,000, suggesting they are functionally dormant and represent passive rather than active financial participation.

This paper addresses the fundamental gap between these two realities. The core research question is direct: why does a country with 88% digital transaction volume by channel share still operate as a predominantly cash economy? The answer, this paper argues, lies not in infrastructure deficiency; the infrastructure exists, is deployed, and processes substantial transaction volume but in what we term the "behavioral last mile," the compounding demand-side barriers that persist after infrastructure is in place and operational.

Most academic and policy literature on digital payment adoption focuses on the supply side: building payment rails, deploying terminals, licensing wallets, establishing regulatory frameworks (Demirguc-Kunt et al., 2022). The demand side; the behavioral, institutional, and structural reasons people choose not to use infrastructure that exists receives far less systematic analysis in the literature. Pakistan is the ideal case study because the paradox is quantified with precision, the data is publicly available from credible regulatory and research sources, and the tension between infrastructure success and behavioral stasis is acute and measurable.

The analysis is structured around three compounding layers of the behavioral last mile: trust deficit, incentive misalignment, and literacy friction. Each layer is examined with Pakistan-specific evidence and compared against India (UPI) and Kenya (M-Pesa) at equivalent infrastructure stages. The paper concludes with five specific interventions derived from the comparative analysis, each with implementation guidance and precedent from comparable markets.

2. Literature Review

The literature on digital payment adoption in developing economies clusters around two broad themes: infrastructure deployment as a driver of financial inclusion, and behavioral barriers to adoption. The gap lies in their intersection; what happens when infrastructure is deployed at scale but adoption stalls at inadequate levels.

On the supply side, the World Bank's Global Findex database has documented the link between account ownership and infrastructure availability across six survey waves since 2011 (Demirguc-Kunt et al., 2022). The consensus finding is robust and consistent across contexts: infrastructure availability is necessary but not sufficient for meaningful adoption. Account ownership reached 79% globally by 2025, yet usage patterns vary enormously across comparable economies with similar infrastructure levels. This variance points to demand-side rather than supply-side explanations for adoption divergence.

The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), introduced by Davis (1989), provides the foundational theoretical lens for understanding digital service adoption. TAM posits that adoption depends on two primary factors: perceived usefulness (belief that the system enhances job or life performance) and perceived ease of use (belief that the system is user-friendly). Subsequent extensions incorporate trust as a critical mediating variable, particularly relevant in contexts where institutional credibility is low (Gefen et al., 2003). In Pakistan's context, trust is not merely a mediating variable; it is a primary structural barrier to adoption.

Kahneman and Tversky's (1979) prospect theory provides the lens for understanding why cash persistence persists despite digital infrastructure. Prospect theory demonstrates that individuals weight losses more heavily than equivalent gains—a loss of cash (handing money to a merchant) feels more significant than the gain of convenience (digital transaction). This loss aversion bias explains why many individuals, even when digital infrastructure is available, continue to default to the cognitive and behavioral patterns that feel less risky.

Rogers's (2003) diffusion of innovations framework explains why technology adoption is not automatic upon deployment. The diffusion process depends on five factors: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability. In Pakistan's payment ecosystem, digital systems score well on relative advantage (speed, security) and trialability (low cost to experiment), but poorly on compatibility (conflicting with existing cash-based workflows) and observability (users cannot easily see transaction status or validate that digital payment actually occurred without digital confirmation literacy).

3. Methodology and Data Sources

This study employs comparative case study methodology to isolate behavioral variables from infrastructure variables. Pakistan is the primary case, examined through quantitative data from two authoritative sources:

  • State Bank of Pakistan (2025): Annual Payment Systems Review FY25 (verified data on transaction volumes, merchant networks, agent counts, and channel shares)
  • Karandaaz Pakistan (2025): Pakistan Financial Inclusion Survey (K-FIS) 2024 (demand-side survey data on trust, literacy, financial inclusion, and demographic patterns)

Comparison cases are analyzed at equivalent infrastructure stages. India's Unified Payment Interface (UPI) launched in April 2016; by April 2021 (year 5), it processed 8.7 billion transactions and reached 62% digital payment adoption among urban populations (NPCI, 2025). Kenya's M-Pesa launched in March 2007; by March 2012 (year 5), it reached 42% adult mobile money adoption and had 40,000+ agents (Central Bank of Kenya, 2012; Jack & Suri, 2014).

Pakistan's Raast launched in January 2021; by January 2026 (year 5), it processed 1.28 billion transactions with 88% digital channel share by volume, yet financial inclusion remains at 35%, the lowest of the three cases at equivalent stages. This comparison isolates behavioral adoption as the dependent variable while holding infrastructure deployment constant.

All quantitative claims in this paper are cross-referenced against original source documents. No data has been estimated, inferred, or extrapolated. All figures are derived from official regulatory publications.

4. Pakistan's Digital Payment Landscape

Pakistan's payment infrastructure has transformed dramatically in five years. Raast, launched in January 2021 as the country's first instant payment system, processed 1,276 million peer-to-peer transactions worth PKR 29.6 trillion in FY25, compared to 147.2 million transactions worth PKR 3.1 trillion in FY23 (State Bank of Pakistan, 2025). This represents 8.7x growth in transaction volume over two years. The branchless banking agent network expanded to 731,814 agents, providing physical access points for account holders throughout the country. POS terminals reached 195,849 across 159,284 merchants, a 56% increase from the prior year. QR-enabled merchants doubled from 516,317 to 1,092,004. Mobile banking app users grew from 18.7 million to 24.1 million. Branchless banking mobile app users reached 79.2 million, up from 58.7 million, representing 35% growth.

The digital share of retail transactions climbed steadily from 78% in FY23 to 85% in FY24 to 88% in FY25 by transaction volume (State Bank of Pakistan, 2025). Total retail payment transactions reached 9.1 billion annually, valued at PKR 612 trillion cumulatively. Mobile banking applications alone processed 6.2 billion transactions, growing 52% year over year, representing the fastest-growing transaction channel.

These numbers unambiguously establish that infrastructure is not the bottleneck in Pakistan's payment ecosystem. The rails exist. The merchants are onboarded. The apps are downloaded and actively used. The question is therefore not why infrastructure is absent but why this infrastructure produces a cash-dominant economy rather than a digitally transacting one.

The answer begins with a critical distinction that headline numbers obscure: the 88% digital share is by transaction volume, not by transaction value. By value, the digital share is approximately 29% (State Bank of Pakistan Q2 FY25 quarterly data). High-volume, low-value digital transactions (peer-to-peer transfers, airtime top-ups, utility bill payments) inflate the volume share while cash continues to dominate high-value economic activity (retail commerce, wholesale trade, real estate transactions, informal market transactions). The 98% ATM cash withdrawal rate confirms this pattern: people use digital channels for transfers and bill payments but withdraw cash for actual spending. This reveals that digital infrastructure carries small-value, low-friction transactions while cash carries the economically significant transactions.

5. Layer 1: Trust Deficit

The Karandaaz K-FIS 2024 provides the most granular trust data available for Pakistan's financial services sector. Among adults excluded from the formal financial system, only 9% trust banks and 8% trust mobile money providers (Karandaaz, 2025). Only 36% of all adults report comfort using any financial service. These figures are not marginal variations in a generally trusting population; they describe a population-level trust deficit affecting approximately two-thirds of the adult population.

The roots of this distrust are structural and historically grounded. Pakistan's banking sector has historically served an urban, affluent minority. Branch networks concentrate in cities and metropolitan areas, leaving rural areas underserved. Account opening procedures require documentation (national identity cards, proof of address, tax identification) that many informal workers and rural populations lack. The 2008 banking crisis and periodic currency controls reinforced the perception that formal financial institutions are unreliable custodians of money. For the 85% of excluded adults who rely on family and friends for credit (Karandaaz, 2025), the informal system is not a fallback option—it is the primary system, and it functions on trust relationships built through repeated interaction and social obligation that formal institutions cannot replicate in short timeframes.

Cash transactions offer anonymity and immediacy. For the estimated 40% of Pakistan's GDP that operates in the informal economy, digital transactions create a permanent record trail that many small business owners perceive as tax exposure risk. This perception is not irrational behavior or unfounded fear; it is a rational response to an incentive structure where formalization carries direct costs (documentation burdens, taxation obligations, compliance requirements) without proportionate benefits for small operators. Formalizing means higher tax liability without corresponding increase in customer demand, quality of service, or market opportunity.

The comparison with Kenya is instructive and illuminating. M-Pesa's adoption in Kenya overcame significant institutional distrust through a specific mechanism: the agent network. M-Pesa agents were existing shopkeepers embedded in local trust networks (Jack & Suri, 2014), individuals who had already established community trust through years of business operation. Users did not need to trust Safaricom (the telecom provider) or the banking system; they trusted the person behind the counter whom they had known for years. By 2012, five years after launch, Kenya had developed over 40,000 M-Pesa agents, and 42% of adults held mobile money accounts (World Bank Findex, 2011). Trust was not built through institution-building but through leveraging existing social institutions.

Pakistan's branchless banking agent network is numerically larger than Kenya's (731,814 agents by FY25 vs. Kenya's peak of 40,000), but it serves a population roughly four times Kenya's size, giving substantially lower per-capita agent density. More importantly, the agent network primarily facilitates cash-in/cash-out transactions (converting digital money back to physical cash), not digital payment facilitation. Agents are access points for the cash economy, not bridges to the digital one. Raast, meanwhile, is entirely app-based. It requires a smartphone, an internet connection, and a bank account;all of which presuppose a level of institutional engagement and technological comfort that the trust-deficit population does not possess.

6. Layer 2: Incentive Misalignment

Even among individuals who have overcome the trust barrier sufficiently to maintain bank accounts and smartphone access, a second barrier emerges: merchants face economic disincentives to accept digital payments. Pakistan's payment ecosystem imposes a Merchant Discount Rate (MDR); a fee charged to the merchant for accepting digital payments—that ranges from 1.5% to 2.5% depending on transaction channel, merchant category, and payment instrument. This means that a shopkeeper accepting a digital payment of PKR 1,000 receives only PKR 975–985, while a cash transaction carries zero fee. Over a year of thousands of transactions, this differential compounds into substantial lost revenue.

For small merchants (the backbone of Pakistan's retail economy), this fee structure creates a structural barrier to digital payment acceptance. A shopkeeper with monthly sales of PKR 2 million paying 2% MDR incurs annual costs of PKR 480,000 (approximately USD 1,700) simply to accept digital payments. This cost is not offset by equivalent benefits: digital payment acceptance does not increase customer demand, does not command price premiums, and does not reduce operational costs for small businesses that lack the scale for POS efficiency. The merchant therefore rationally defaults to cash and relies on customers to bring cash to the transaction.

India's Reserve Bank of India implemented zero MDR for UPI and RuPay debit card transactions in December 2019, effective January 1, 2020. The policy applied zero fees to merchants accepting RuPay and UPI payments, eliminating the cost barrier entirely. The results were immediate: UPI transactions jumped from 8.7 billion in 2019 to 228.3 billion in 2025, a 26-fold increase (NPCI, 2025). Pakistan's Raast operates under the standard MDR framework. No comparable zero-fee policy has been implemented for Raast merchants.

This incentive structure cascades through the payment ecosystem. Merchants who do not accept digital payments—the majority of Pakistan's informal retail sector—have no incentive to educate customers about digital payment options. Customers who find that most merchants only accept cash have no incentive to attempt digital payment. The network effect that enabled India's dramatic UPI scaling does not emerge in Pakistan because the merchant incentive structure is fundamentally misaligned with digital adoption.

7. Layer 3: Literacy Friction

The third compounding barrier is digital and financial literacy friction. Pakistan's smartphone penetration (45% of adults) outpaces digital payment adoption (30%), indicating that device availability is not the binding constraint. However, gender and rural disparities are acute. Among women, smartphone ownership is only 46%, compared to 82% for men (Karandaaz, 2025). In rural areas, smartphone penetration drops below 35%. Digital literacy (the ability to navigate digital payment applications, understand security practices, validate transaction confirmation) lags far behind device ownership.

Most digital payment applications in Pakistan are designed in English, despite English literacy in rural areas being below 15%. Urdu language support is available in many applications but relegated to secondary status in user interface design. For users who have not interacted with digital systems regularly, the cognitive load of navigation compounds with language barriers to create friction that cash transactions do not require. Cash requires only the ability to count currency and recognize denominations—skills that are context-independent and acquired early in childhood.

The agent network in Pakistan is underdeveloped for digital payment education. Unlike Kenya's M-Pesa agents, who actively facilitate transactions and explain the system to users, Pakistan's branchless banking agents are primarily cash-handling points. They are not incentivized or trained to educate customers about digital payment functionality. The result is that despite 731,814 agent touchpoints, the literacy transfer mechanism that was critical to M-Pesa adoption is absent.

Feature phone users (individuals with devices but without smartphones) are excluded entirely from digital payment participation in Pakistan. Raast and most digital payment systems require either a smartphone app or internet-enabled device. India's UPI ecosystem includes USSD-based payment options that allow feature phone users to participate in digital payments through simple text-based interface commands. Pakistan has not implemented equivalent feature-phone access mechanisms, effectively creating a digital divide between smartphone owners and the broader population.

8. Conclusions and Policy Interventions

This analysis identifies that Pakistan's cash persistence is not attributable to infrastructure deficiency but to three compounding demand-side barriers: trust deficit, incentive misalignment, and literacy friction. These barriers are not independent variables; they compound multiplicatively. An individual who distrusts banks has no incentive to overcome literacy friction to use digital payments. A merchant who perceives digital payment fees erodes profitability has no incentive to promote digital payment adoption to customers. A customer who finds few merchants accept digital payments has no incentive to invest in digital literacy.

The evidence from comparative cases—India's zero MDR policy and Kenya's agent-based trust architecture demonstrates that targeted behavioral policy interventions can accelerate adoption dramatically without requiring additional infrastructure investment. Both interventions worked within existing infrastructure, modifying incentive structures or leveraging social trust mechanisms to remove behavioral barriers.

Five specific interventions are proposed for Pakistan, each with precedent from comparable markets:

  1. Eliminate merchant discount rates for transactions below PKR 5,000. Precedent: India's zero MDR policy. Implementation: State Bank of Pakistan policy mandate requiring acquirers to subsidize MDR for small-value transactions, with government fiscal compensation if necessary. Expected outcome: Dramatically increase merchant incentive to accept digital payments for daily commerce transactions.
  2. Redesign Raast and payment system architecture to support feature phone access via USSD/IVR. Precedent: UPI USSD implementation in India. Implementation: Develop text-based command interface for feature phone users (approximately 40% of population remains feature-phone dependent). Expected outcome: Extend digital payment participation to individuals without smartphone access, reducing digital divide.
  3. Deploy trust intermediaries with aligned incentives for digital facilitation. Precedent: M-Pesa agent network architecture. Implementation: Restructure branchless banking agent incentive models to compensate agents for digital payment facilitation (transaction-based commissions for merchant and customer digital enrollments, not just cash-handling). Expected outcome: Convert access network into adoption network by creating economic incentive for digital education and transaction execution.
  4. Create tax incentives for documented digital spending. Precedent: Brazil's Pix design elements, Pakistan's existing CNIC-linked formal sector incentives. Implementation: Allow individual filers to claim 1–2% tax deduction on documented digital spending, reframing formalization from cost to benefit. Expected outcome: Reduce tax evasion concern and reframe digital participation as economically advantageous for small business owners.
  5. Mandate Urdu-first interface design standards for all financial applications. Precedent: European Union digital accessibility standards, India's regional language emphasis in fintech applications. Implementation: SBP regulatory requirement for all licensed payment applications to provide Urdu as primary language option in user interface design. Expected outcome: Reduce literacy friction, particularly for women and rural populations, by removing language as barrier to system navigation.

These interventions are not mutually exclusive. Maximum impact would result from coordinated implementation of all five, addressing the three demand-side barriers simultaneously. None require new infrastructure investment. All can be implemented within existing regulatory frameworks. All have demonstrated effectiveness in comparable emerging market contexts.

The central insight of this analysis is straightforward but consequential: infrastructure deployment is necessary but insufficient for digital payment adoption at scale. The 88% digital transaction share by volume proves infrastructure competence. The 35% financial inclusion and 60% cash economy dominance prove that behavioral barriers are the binding constraints. Policy design must shift from supply-side infrastructure expansion to demand-side barrier elimination. The tools exist, the evidence is clear, and the precedent from comparable markets is available. Implementation requires only deliberate policy attention to behavioral economics alongside infrastructure engineering.
