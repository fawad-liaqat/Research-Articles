Tokenized Remittances: A Feasibility Framework for Pakistani Diaspora Investment

Fawad Liaqat March 2026 18 min read
Abstract

Pakistan receives over $35 billion annually in remittances, overwhelmingly consumed rather than invested. Asset tokenization enables fractional ownership and cross-border investment in Singapore, Dubai, and Switzerland. This analysis asks whether tokenization can redirect a fraction of Pakistani diaspora remittance flows into productive investment capital — and whether current SBP and SECP regulations permit it. Through regulatory mapping of the SBP Foreign Exchange Manual, SECP sandbox framework, and the Pakistan Virtual Assets Act 2026, combined with international precedent analysis from MAS, DFSA, and FINMA, we find that a compliant tokenized remittance investment product is not feasible under current regulations. Three specific regulatory blockers are identified. However, the SECP sandbox pathway offers a viable testing ground, and a phased implementation timeline is proposed. Market sizing across three scenarios suggests addressable volumes of $350 million to $3.5 billion annually, depending on regulatory clarity and product accessibility.

1. Introduction

Pakistan received $34.9 billion in remittances in FY2025. Saudi Arabia led with $9.34 billion, followed by the United Arab Emirates at $7.83 billion, United Kingdom at $5.99 billion, and the United States at $3.72 billion. These flows are overwhelmingly consumed — food, housing, education — rather than invested. Meanwhile, asset tokenization is enabling fractional ownership globally. Singapore's Monetary Authority launched Project Guardian. Dubai completed its first tokenized property transaction in 2025. Switzerland's DLT Act created the world's most mature framework for tokenized securities.

The question is straightforward: can tokenization redirect even a small fraction of Pakistan's remittance flows into productive investment capital? And can this be done within the constraints of Pakistan's regulatory framework? This article examines feasibility across three dimensions: regulatory viability through analysis of the SBP Foreign Exchange Manual, SECP sandbox framework, and Pakistan Virtual Assets Act 2026; market sizing using three scenarios anchored in precedent data; and technical architecture design for a compliant product. The conclusion is honest: the product is not feasible today under current regulations. But the path to feasibility is identifiable and narrower than it appears.

2. Regulatory Landscape

2.1 SBP Foreign Exchange Manual Constraints

The Foreign Exchange Manual is the authoritative source for permissible foreign exchange operations. Chapter 19 requires all inward remittances to be processed through authorized dealers — commercial banks, development finance institutions, and microfinance banks. No provision exists for intermediary platforms to intercept remittance flows before PKR conversion. This is the most binding constraint for any tokenized remittance product.

Chapter 20 governs capital account operations for non-residents. Non-residents can invest in government securities, including Pakistan Investment Bonds and Treasury Bills, sukuk (Islamic bonds), and listed equities on the Pakistan Stock Exchange. However, tokenized assets are not recognized as an investment category under the existing framework. Chapter 1 permits foreign currency accounts under the Roshan Digital Account scheme, allowing USD holding before conversion — but this does not extend to diversion toward unregulated investment products or third-party intermediaries.

The critical precedent is Naya Pakistan Certificates, launched in 2020 via Roshan Digital Account. NPCs attracted approximately $3.1 billion in gross inflows from roughly 500,000 accounts by the end of 2023, proving that diaspora investment demand exists. However, NPCs succeeded because they were government-backed and SBP-regulated — not intermediary-led platforms. This distinction is legally material.

2.2 SECP Sandbox and Pakistan Virtual Assets Act 2026

The Securities and Exchange Commission of Pakistan Regulatory Sandbox, launched in 2019, has approved real estate tokenization in its second cohort (2025). The Pakistan Virtual Assets Ordinance 2025 established the Pakistan Virtual Asset Regulatory Authority as an autonomous federal regulator, and the Virtual Assets Act 2026 passed both chambers of parliament in March 2026. These developments represent genuine regulatory modernization.

However, a critical gap remains: no stablecoin-specific framework exists. The SBP has not authorized stablecoin operations, driven by legitimate capital flight concerns under the International Monetary Fund programme. The Virtual Assets Act creates licensing for exchanges, custody providers, broker-dealers, and derivatives platforms — but stablecoins, which are how Pakistan's crypto economy actually functions, remain in regulatory limbo. This creates a circular problem: tokenized remittance products require stable-value tokens for settlement, but stablecoins cannot legally operate.

The SECP sandbox pathway is the most viable route for a pilot product. An application would require detailed product description, comprehensive risk assessment, compliance plan, technology audit, and consumer protection measures. The SECP has demonstrated willingness to test novel structures — real estate tokenization approval proves this. Sandbox approvals typically provide 12-18 months of supervised operation before commercial licensure.

2.3 International Precedents and Regulatory Frameworks

Singapore — MAS Project Guardian: The Monetary Authority of Singapore represents the gold standard for supervised tokenization experimentation. The November 2025 Operational Guide for Tokenized Funds provides detailed governance, net asset value calculation, and compliance frameworks. In 2026, MAS launched a pilot for tokenized government bills settled via wholesale central bank digital currency with DBS Bank, JPMorgan Chase, and Standard Chartered. Key lesson: MAS allowed controlled experimentation before commercial deployment, with clear success metrics and regulatory feedback loops.

Dubai — DFSA Crypto Token Framework: The updated Crypto Token framework (January 2026) transfers suitability assessment responsibility to authorized firms rather than imposing blanket restrictions. Dubai completed its first tokenized property transaction in 2025, demonstrating practical implementation. The Dubai Financial Services Authority applies identical regulatory standards to digital assets and traditional financial services. Key lesson: integrated regulation rather than parallel regimes, with clear institutional accountability.

Switzerland — FINMA DLT Act: Switzerland provides the most mature framework globally. FINMA classifies tokens as payment tokens, utility tokens, or asset tokens, with asset tokens regulated under existing securities law. BX Digital received a FINMA license as a DLT trading venue in 2025. Key lesson: technology-neutral regulation — existing laws adapted for blockchain rather than creating parallel frameworks. This reduces compliance complexity and regulatory uncertainty.

3. Market Sizing and Demand Analysis

3.1 Remittance Flow Data and Geographic Distribution

Pakistan's FY2025 remittances reached $34.9 billion, representing a 6.2 percent increase from FY2024. The five largest corridors account for approximately 78 percent of total inflows: Saudi Arabia ($9.34 billion), United Arab Emirates ($7.83 billion), United Kingdom ($5.99 billion), United States ($3.72 billion), and Qatar (estimated $1.5 billion from broader GCC category). These corridors are characterized by concentrated diaspora populations with relatively stable employment in professional services, healthcare, and skilled trades.

Cost comparison analysis demonstrates significant fee savings through alternative channels. Mobile wallet providers average 2.16 percent all-in cost versus 11.32 percent for traditional banks, suggesting potential savings of $370-385 million annually if 10 percent of flows migrated. However, fee savings alone do not predict investment behavior — remittance recipients optimize for speed and reliability rather than cost minimization, as demonstrated by continued banking channel usage despite higher fees.

Optimistic scenario assumes regulatory clarification, banking partnership distribution, and emergence of multiple asset classes. Singapore's tokenized fund pilot reached $1.2 billion in committed capital within six months of launch (Q2-Q3 2026). Applying 60 percent of that growth rate to Pakistan's $35 billion remittance base yields $2.45-3.5 billion in annual redirection.

4. Technical Architecture

4.1 System Design Overview

The proposed architecture addresses the regulatory constraints identified above while maintaining operational efficiency and compliance auditability. Architecture design prioritizes regulatory surveillance capability, investor identity verification at protocol level, and institutional custody integration.

Blockchain Layer: A permissioned or hybrid blockchain (Hyperledger Besu or Polygon with permissioning modules) is most credible given the SECP's likely requirement for transaction auditability and KYC enforcement at the protocol level. A public permissionless blockchain like Ethereum would not satisfy regulatory requirements for investor identification, transaction monitoring, and intervention capability. Permissioned architecture enables regulators to audit full transaction history and intervene if necessary.

Token Standard: ERC-1400 (security token standard with transfer restrictions) or ERC-3643/T-REX (identity-verified transfer standard) are appropriate for this use case. Plain ERC-20 tokens are insufficient because they lack built-in transfer restrictions, investor whitelisting, and compliance hooks required for regulated securities. ERC-1400 supports forced transfers (required for regulatory intervention in case of fraud), document management, and partition-based ownership structures.

KYC/AML Integration: Integration with NADRA VERISYS API for identity verification of Pakistani nationals. Enhanced due diligence for non-resident investors per Financial Action Task Force standards. On-chain identity attestation linked to smart contract whitelisting ensures only verified investors can transact. Compliance-as-code architecture enforces regulatory rules at the smart contract level rather than relying on operational controls alone.

Custody and Settlement: A SECP-licensed custodian holds underlying assets — real estate titles, sukuk certificates, fund units. Token ownership maps to beneficial ownership recorded in the custodian's books. Legal ownership structure follows existing Pakistani trust law framework. This hybrid architecture maintains compliance with banking regulation while enabling fractionalization benefits.

Fiat On/Off-Ramp Constraint: This represents the most binding technical constraint. The on-ramp requires a licensed authorized dealer to accept remittance funds and convert to investment token units. The off-ramp requires PKR conversion through authorized channels per SBP Foreign Exchange Manual Chapter 19. Both require explicit SBP authorization that does not currently exist. This is the primary blocker for any real-world implementation today.

4.2 Eligible Asset Classes

Four asset classes emerge as most viable for initial tokenization. Real estate — fractional ownership of commercial property in Pakistan or diaspora-accessible jurisdictions — has the strongest regulatory precedent given the SECP's 2025 sandbox approval of real estate tokenization. Infrastructure assets, including toll roads and power generation facilities, represent the second-most viable category given their long-term cash flow stability. Sukuk and other Islamic bonds provide familiarity advantage and alignment with diaspora religious preferences. Managed funds — multi-asset portfolios managed by Pakistani or licensed international managers — offer diversification and reduce single-asset concentration risk.

Real estate tokenization in premium markets (Dubai, Singapore, London) offers particular appeal to diaspora investors seeking portfolio diversification beyond Pakistan while maintaining cultural/geographic proximity. A pilot could target UAE commercial property, benefiting from DFSA regulatory clarity and existing diaspora real estate demand.

5. Feasibility Assessment and Regulatory Timeline

5.1 Current Feasibility

Is a tokenized remittance investment product feasible today? The answer is no. Three specific regulatory blockers prevent implementation: (1) SBP Foreign Exchange Manual Chapter 19 requires all remittances through authorized dealers with no provision for intermediary diversion; (2) no stablecoin framework exists despite the Virtual Assets Act 2026, driven by SBP capital flight concerns under the IMF programme; (3) tokenized assets are not recognized as an investment category under Foreign Exchange Manual Chapter 20, creating legal uncertainty about non-resident eligibility.

These blockers are not insurmountable but require coordinated policy action. The International Monetary Fund programme constraint is temporary — it expires in 2027. The stablecoin framework is under development at PVARA. The investment category gap is a documentation issue rather than a conceptual obstacle.

5.2 Regulatory Pathway to Feasibility

What would enable implementation? Four policy actions are required: (1) SBP circular authorizing licensed fintechs as authorized dealers for tokenized investment products, with strict remittance-to-investment conversion rules and lock-up periods; (2) PVARA stablecoin regulations establishing operational frameworks pending SBP coordination; (3) SECP sandbox approval for a tokenized remittance investment pilot with 12-18 month testing period; (4) amendment to Foreign Exchange Manual Chapter 20 recognizing tokenized securities as eligible non-resident investment instruments.

SECP sandbox represents the lowest-friction entry point. The SECP has already demonstrated regulatory capability and willingness through real estate tokenization approval. A sandbox application can proceed independently of broader stablecoin framework questions by using regulated stablecoins (USDC, USDT) held in escrow by a licensed custodian, converting to PKR only at investor exit.

5.3 Implementation Timeline

Phase 1 (2026-2027): SECP sandbox application and pilot approval. Estimated 6-9 month approval cycle based on real estate tokenization precedent. Product scope limited to two asset classes (real estate and sukuk). Target: 5,000 investor accounts. Regulatory focus: operational controls, custody safeguards, consumer protection.

Phase 2 (2027-2028): Pilot execution with continuous regulatory reporting. Quarterly reports to SECP on investor behavior, redemption patterns, custody efficiency, and compliance metrics. Addition of third asset class (managed funds) based on pilot performance. Target: 20,000-50,000 investor accounts.

Phase 3 (2028-2029): Regulatory amendments based on pilot data. SBP circular authorizing fintechs as authorized dealers. Foreign Exchange Manual Chapter 20 amendment. Transition from sandbox to commercial licensure. Target: 100,000+ investor accounts; $1.5-2.5 billion annual investment volume.

Comparison with International Timelines: Singapore took three years from Project Guardian launch (2022) to commercial pilot (2025-2026). Dubai's DFSA framework evolved over four years (2022-2026). Pakistan could follow a similar 3-4 year trajectory if regulatory engagement begins in 2026. This assumes sustained institutional coordination between SECP and SBP.

6. Conclusion

The opportunity is real and quantifiable. Pakistan's $35 billion annual remittance flow represents an underutilized capital source for productive investment. Even at a conservative 1-2 percent redirection rate, the addressable market is $350-700 million annually — comparable to Naya Pakistan Certificate's gross inflows during its peak years.

The regulatory path is narrow but not closed. The SECP has already approved real estate tokenization, demonstrating capability. The Pakistan Virtual Assets Act 2026 creates institutional infrastructure (PVARA) that was absent before. The Roshan Digital Account precedent proves diaspora investors will engage with regulated Pakistani investment products. Pakistan could follow the MAS model: supervised experimentation in a sandbox, data-driven policy refinement, and phased commercial deployment.

What is missing is political will and institutional coordination between SBP and SECP. The SBP's capital flight concerns are legitimate and must be addressed through product design — custody requirements, lock-up periods, and conversion caps — rather than blanket prohibition. The alternative is continued regulatory ambiguity while $35 billion flows through the economy each year with minimal productive investment capture. The question is not whether tokenized diaspora investment products will exist. It is whether Pakistan will be an early mover or a late adopter — and whether the regulatory framework will be shaped by deliberate design or by default.
