Why CBDCs Fail at the Interface: Design Lessons from the eNaira and Sand Dollar for Pakistan's Digital Rupee

Fawad Liaqat | March 2026

Introduction

Ninety-eight point five percent. That is the share of eNaira wallets that went unused within one year of Nigeria's central bank digital currency launch, according to IMF Working Paper WP/23/104 published in May 2023. The Central Bank of Nigeria had spent years designing the architecture, selecting a technology partner (Bitt Inc.), building a tiered KYC framework, and marketing the product to 38 million unbanked Nigerians. The policy rationale was sound. The macroeconomic case was defensible. The technology worked.

The users never came.

This article argues that the eNaira's failure and the Sand Dollar's stalled adoption in the Bahamas are not policy failures or technology failures. They are design failures. Specifically, they are failures of user experience: onboarding friction, language barriers, absent offline capability, opaque error handling, and a fundamental disconnection between the product and the habits of its intended users. The pattern matters for Pakistan because the State Bank of Pakistan entered its own CBDC pilot in September 2025, partnering with Soramitsu to build a Digital Rupee targeting over 100 million unbanked adults. If SBP treats the interface as an afterthought — as Nigeria and the Bahamas did — the outcome is predictable.

This analysis conducts a structured UX audit of the eNaira and Sand Dollar across five design dimensions, then derives a set of requirements for the Digital Rupee that are grounded in Pakistani demographic data from PBS, Karandaaz, and PTA. The goal is specificity: not generic CBDC recommendations, but requirements that embed Pakistan's particular constraints; 39% illiteracy, 91% institutional distrust among the excluded, 36.5% rural internet penetration into the product design itself.

Methodology Note

The audit relies entirely on observable evidence. Neither the Central Bank of Nigeria nor the Central Bank of the Bahamas publishes internal UX design documentation, and no institutional access was available for this analysis. The evidence base consists of: the IMF's independent evaluation of the eNaira (WP/23/104); academic text mining and sentiment analysis of eNaira Speed Wallet app reviews (SSRN, Obiora et al.; African Journal of Science, Technology, Innovation and Development, 2024); BIS publications (Papers No. 128, 147, and the CGIDE Report); Central Bank of the Bahamas press releases and documentation; journalistic and third-party analysis from CoinDesk, Carnegie Endowment, The Conversation, and others; and demographic data from the Pakistan Bureau of Statistics (2023 Census), Karandaaz K-FIS 2024, and PTA Annual Report 2024-25.

This evidence base is sufficient to identify systematic design failures but insufficient to evaluate internal decision-making processes or to assess unreleased design iterations. The Sand Dollar evidence is thinner than the eNaira's — partly because the Bahamas has a population of 400,000 compared to Nigeria's 220 million, producing less academic and journalistic attention. Where evidence is unavailable, the audit says so rather than speculating.

The eNaira Audit

Onboarding friction

Onboarding friction was the eNaira's most immediate failure. The Speed Wallet app required email verification, BVN or NIN authentication, and Terms & Conditions acceptance before any transaction was possible. Academic analysis of app reviews identified onboarding as the most frequent complaint category: email verification locked users out; BVN authentication returned errors for valid credentials; and NIN verification through NIMC (Nigeria's identity authority) was systemically unreliable. The severity of this last point cannot be overstated: 7.5 million MTN subscribers were disconnected due to NIN verification failures, indicating that the identity infrastructure the eNaira depended on was itself broken. The app was removed from Google Play 36 hours after launch with a 1.9-star initial rating. CBN had designed a Tier 0 wallet (phone-only, N20,000 daily limit) to bypass identity verification, but user reports indicate NIN or BVN was still required in practice. The theoretical inclusion pathway never reached the population it was designed for.

Language and localization

Language and localization compounded the onboarding problem. The eNaira was available exclusively in English in a country with over 500 languages and three major lingua francas (Hausa, Yoruba, Igbo) beyond English. The interface was text-heavy rather than icon-driven. A financial inclusion product available only in the former colonial language, in a country where the unbanked population predominantly speaks indigenous languages, fails the localization test categorically.

Offline capability

Offline capability was nominal. The USSD code *997# provided feature-phone access, but USSD requires cellular signal — it is not true offline. Users in areas without cellular coverage could not transact at all. No stored-value or NFC mechanism was deployed. In a country with unreliable electricity and significant data costs, the product was unavailable precisely when and where users needed it most.

Error handling

Error handling was destructive. App review analysis reveals transaction failures with no explanation, registration errors that were permanently marked in the system (blocking retry), excessive customer support wait times, and no confirmation that funds were safe during failed transactions. For a population that already distrusted digital finance, a single unexplained error was sufficient cause for permanent abandonment. The data confirms this: 90% of users who created wallets never completed more than a handful of transactions.

Integration with existing habits

Integration with existing habits was absent. The eNaira required downloading a standalone app, creating a new account, and verifying identity through a separate system — all to access a payment channel that competed with existing apps (OPay, PalmPay, bank apps) offering identical or superior functionality. A Springer study found that 49% of participants stated they would not use the eNaira because existing alternatives were sufficient. India's Digital Rupee, by contrast, distributed through Google Pay and PhonePe — apps people already had — and achieved 6 million users with 334% year-over-year circulation growth.

The Sand Dollar Audit

The Sand Dollar presents a more nuanced picture. Its consumer onboarding is the lowest-friction in any CBDC: Tier 1 wallets require no identity documents at all, up to a $500 balance and $1,500 monthly transaction limit. This design decision is correct for inclusion purposes. However, merchant onboarding failed — businesses rejected separate POS devices and demanded compatibility with existing terminals. The authorized financial institutions that were supposed to implement ACH wallet integration never fully did so. The result is a product with easy entry and nowhere useful to go.

The Sand Dollar's offline capability is the comparison's standout. Pre-loaded value from ATMs allows transactions without network connectivity, with deferred reconciliation upon reconnection. This mechanism was built because hurricanes routinely destroy Bahamian telecommunications infrastructure. It is the only production-tested offline CBDC mechanism in existence, and it demonstrates that the feature is technically feasible without exotic hardware.

Language is not a barrier (English is the Bahamas' sole language), and error handling evidence is insufficient for assessment. The integration problem is similar to Nigeria's but less severe: the physical payment card maps to existing habits, even though the wallet apps are standalone. Adoption metrics remain poor — 138,000 wallets in a 400,000 population, less than 1% of currency in circulation — and digital wallet top-up transactions declined 78% year-over-year in 2024. The Sand Dollar is not a catastrophic failure like the eNaira, but it is a stalled product with a merchant acceptance problem it has not solved.

Pakistan's Demographic Context

The Digital Rupee's target user is not an abstraction. PBS 2023 Census data, Karandaaz K-FIS 2024, and PTA's Annual Report allow us to construct a realistic profile.

Pakistan has 241.5 million people. Sixty-one percent live in rural areas. National literacy is 60.65%, but drops to 51.56% in rural areas and lower still for rural women (approximately 40%). The median age is 19 — this is a young population, but youth does not automatically mean digital fluency in a country where rural internet penetration is 36.5%.

K-FIS 2024 reports 35% financial inclusion; a fourfold increase from 8% in 2013, driven almost entirely by mobile money (JazzCash, Easypaisa). But the gender gap is severe: 56% of men hold accounts versus 14% of women, a 42 percentage point difference. More critically, only 9% of financially excluded adults trust banks, and 8% trust mobile money providers. This is not apathy. It is active distrust of the institutions the Digital Rupee would operate through.

PTA data shows 200 million mobile subscriptions and 81% 3G/4G coverage nationally. But these national figures obscure regional realities: Balochistan and rural Sindh have significantly lower coverage, and Pakistan experiences frequent power outages that render connectivity statistics meaningless during blackouts. The branchless banking agent network (763,000 registered, 287,000 active) is the physical distribution infrastructure that reaches beyond connectivity constraints.

The profile of the hardest adoption case; a rural woman aged 20-35, with approximately 52% probability of literacy, 14% probability of holding any financial account, and 91% probability of distrusting banks — is the population SBP claims to target. Every design requirement must be tested against this profile.

Requirements for the Digital Rupee

The requirements table derives each specification from a specific failure (eNaira or Sand Dollar) combined with a specific Pakistani constraint. Four of five requirements are rated Critical — meaning that without them, the Digital Rupee will replicate eNaira's outcome.

Onboarding

Onboarding must bypass NADRA verification for the lowest wallet tier, exactly as Nigeria's Tier 0 was supposed to bypass NIMC — except it must actually work. The zero-KYC wallet should have a daily limit calibrated to Pakistani daily wages (PKR 800-1,500) and register via USSD or SMS on a feature phone, not through an app download. Identity escalation should be incentive-driven, not mandatory.

Language and localization

Language and localization demands Urdu plus at least Punjabi, Sindhi, and Pashto at launch, with additional languages within six months. Navigation must be icon-driven so that every critical action — send, receive, check balance — can be completed by an illiterate user. This is not a "nice to have." With 39% of the population illiterate, a text-dependent interface excludes nearly 95 million people.

Offline capability

Offline capability must go beyond the Sand Dollar model. Pakistan's agent network (287,000 active agents) should serve as offline loading points — not just ATMs, which are sparse in rural areas. The mechanism must work on 2G and feature phones, with a visual confirmation on reconnection that the transaction completed. A reasonable offline limit is PKR 25,000 (approximately one month of rural household spending).

Error handling

Error handling must treat the first transaction as the product's only chance. Every failure must display the reason in the user's language, confirm fund safety explicitly, and offer a one-tap retry. The system must never permanently block a user after a verification failure. Agent-mediated support — modeled on JazzCash's existing resolution channel — should be the primary support path.

Integration with existing habits

Integration with existing habits is the requirement that subsumes all others. The Digital Rupee must not launch as a standalone app. It must live inside JazzCash and Easypaisa, whose combined 83 million accounts represent the largest existing digital payment user base in Pakistan. RAAST's 838,000 P2M merchants should accept Digital Rupee transactions through existing integration. For non-smartphone users, access must be through existing USSD short codes. And the product must offer at least one capability that existing mobile money cannot — programmable government transfers or reduced remittance corridor costs — to justify its existence.

Conclusion

The eNaira and Sand Dollar provide the same lesson through different failure modes. Nigeria failed on execution: the design was theoretically inclusive but operationally hostile. The Bahamas failed on integration: the product was easy to enter but impossible to use at merchants. Both treated the user interface as subordinate to the monetary architecture. Both achieved adoption rates below 2% of their target populations.

Pakistan's Digital Rupee has structural advantages that neither Nigeria nor the Bahamas had: 83 million existing mobile money accounts, 287,000 active agents, RAAST with 1.9 billion lifetime transactions, and a young population with rising digital familiarity. The infrastructure exists. The question is whether SBP will design a product that earns trust from a population that has not given it yet — or whether the Digital Rupee will become another architecturally sound CBDC that nobody uses.

The evidence from five global pilots is unambiguous. Privacy design predicts adoption. Embedding in existing apps predicts adoption. Offline capability predicts relevance in low-connectivity markets. Standalone launches with surveillance architectures predict failure. SBP has the data. The choice is a design decision, not a policy mystery.
